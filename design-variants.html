3 ways to make SipCheck feel crafted, not generated

Three distinct design worlds — same 3 tabs, same flows, same features. Each fixes the "rough / AI-generated" feel. Swipe the phones sideways. Best viewed on your phone; tap a variant above to jump.

Why the current build reads "AI-generated"

  • Clinical teal on near-black — the default "AI app" palette; reads like a fintech dashboard, not a beer app.
  • mug.fill SF Symbols as placeholders everywhere — the single biggest "unfinished" tell. Gray 320pt photo blocks on the verdict card.
  • 100% stock SF system font, no wordmark, no app-icon identity.
  • Onboarding uses default iOS blue (off-brand from screen one).
  • No depth, no haptics, no celebration — flat two-tone surfaces, static reveals.

The craft playbook (from Apple Design Award winners + Vivino / Yuka / Untappd)

Commit to ONE opinionated worldFlighty borrowed airport boards, Lumy borrowed daylight. Specificity is the line between a point of view and a template.
Make the verdict humanGentler Streak's whole win: turn data into a sentence. Pair TRY IT/SKIP IT/YOUR CALL with one warm reason line.
Color that means somethingLumy/Yuka: let the verdict color wash the whole result screen (green=try, amber=call, muted=skip).
Kill the stock placeholdersReal captured label photo as the hero; a designed fallback — never a gray rectangle with a dim mug.
One signature momentCrouton/Dark Noise: a distinct reveal + matched haptic beats many mediocre animations. This is the scan→verdict beat.
Hand-made details = the last 5%Custom app icon, real wordmark, a beer-glass mark with expressions, an empty-state illustration. Signals a human cared.
Variant 1 — the recommended default

Last Call

"Snap it. We'll tell you if it's worth the pour."

The bartender who already knows what you like. Warm glass-of-lager gold on brown-black, frosted-glass cards over wood-dark surfaces, copy that talks like someone leaning across the bar. The coziest, most on-category world — makes teal-on-black look like a spreadsheet.

Palette

Taproom #17130E Oak #241D16 Raised Oak #2E2519 Lager Gold #E8A427 Cream #F6EFE2 Malt Green #5FA85A Brick #C0463B

How it escapes the AI look

  • Warm gold-on-brown is instantly "a beer app," not a dashboard
  • SF Pro Rounded Heavy becomes a free wordmark identity — no stock-font feel
  • Real label photo + amber line-art fallback kills the gray placeholder
  • Spring verdict reveal + meaning-mapped haptic = a hand-made moment

Typography & identity

Display: SF Pro Rounded, Heavy — verdict word, titles, wordmark, stat numbers (tracking −0.5). Body: SF Pro. All via Dynamic Type text styles.

  • Follow-up sheet inherits the warm world & voice ("Did you actually grab it?")
  • Rating save → malt-green success haptic; new beer slides onto the Journal board
  • Onboarding: gold .tint everywhere, line-art illustrations from slide one

Variant 2 — boldest hook

Verdict

"TRY IT. SKIP IT. YOUR CALL. Stamped."

SipCheck as a fearless one-word review — the verdict is literally a rubber stamp slammed onto the label. Big editorial type, warm-paper blocks on neutral near-black, and an evolved teal kept only as the "seal" accent so long-time users still recognize it. The loudest, most print-inspired world.

Palette

Ink #111214 Slate #1C1E22 Warm Paper #EDE6D8 Stamp Teal #37C9BE Ale Amber #F2A93B Try #3DBE6B Skip #E5484D

How it escapes the AI look

  • Warm-paper blocks on cool dark = a deliberate print contrast, not flat two-tone
  • Oversized condensed/expanded type gives real editorial character
  • The stamp graphic IS the illustration — no clip-art risk, no gray blocks
  • "Stamp thunk" reveal (scale + rotate + rigid haptic) is a signature moment

Typography & identity

Display: SF Pro Expanded Heavy, all-caps, tight tracking — verdict + headers. Body: SF Pro. The verdict lives inside a rubber-stamp seal, ≤3° rotation.

  • Scanning phrases give way to the stamp slamming down + haptic thunk
  • Follow-up = a "file this verdict" sheet with stamp-style chips
  • Saved beers become filed "verdict cards" — Journal reads like a review archive
  • Onboarding: teal .tint + sample stamps on warm paper from slide one

Trade-off

Glass + glow are perf-sensitive and can hurt legibility if overused — keep glass off scrolling lists, amber disciplined. Moody dark-only is hardest in a bright store aisle (the actual use context), and Reduce Transparency users lose the character. Guardrail: verdict + CTA use solid high-contrast fills (not glass), and ship a designed opaque tier for Reduce Transparency — never just "blur off."

Which to pick — the design critic's call

Recommendation: Last Call, with one guardrail. It's the only direction that fixes the most damaging critique of the current app — teal-on-black "feels like a fintech dashboard, not a beer app." Warm gold-on-brown is instantly on-category; SF Pro Rounded gives a distinct wordmark identity for free (no bundled font); it closes all three named gaps (placeholder imagery, off-brand onboarding, missing identity) without inventing features. Most buildable as-specified: no iOS 26 dependency, no data it doesn't have.

Guardrail: treat illustration as post-launch — ship on tinted SF Symbols, add hand-drawn line-art after a real illustration pass. That neutralizes its only real weakness.
